The classifier was the whole safety story: how Claude Fable 5 was built — and broken

Claude Fable 5 is built on the most capable cyber engine of any model alive — the same weights Anthropic otherwise keeps locked away — wrapped in a classifier layer. This is how that wrapper was built, why a jailbreak in it mattered more than one in Opus 4.8, and how it broke.

01 — The Engine

First, the size of what's under the hood

Fable 5 isn't a better Opus. Anthropic calls it a "Mythos-class" model — a new capability tier that sits above the Opus line. The gap is not incremental.

On SWE-bench Pro, a test of hard engineering tasks, it scores 80.3% against Opus 4.8's 69.2% and GPT-5.5's 58.6%. It posts about 95% on SWE-bench Verified, 88.0% on Terminal-Bench 2.1, and leads on knowledge-work and reasoning boards too. And the lead grows the longer and more autonomous the task. The number that traveled: in early testing, Stripe pointed Fable 5 at a 50-million-line codebase and it finished a migration in a single day — work that would have taken a full team more than two months by hand.

Now the part that matters for everything below. On cybersecurity, the raw engine is — in Anthropic's own words — the strongest of any model in the world. That is the engine sitting inside the model you could, for three days, call from a web app.

Classifier Gate
02 — The Architecture

Safety as a wrapper, not a property

Start with the fact that does most of the work — and that most coverage buried: Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 are the same underlying model. Not a bigger sibling and a smaller one. Identical weights, identical training, identical parameter count — two deployment configurations of one model, separated by nothing but safeguards. The only thing standing between the public and the restricted engine is a layer of classifiers.

That resolves the question the benchmarks raise — if the two are identical, how can Fable be "the powerful one"? It can't, not next to Mythos. Every time this piece (or anyone) calls Fable powerful, the comparison is to Opus 4.8, the previous-tier model it falls back to. Fable is never stronger than Mythos. It is Mythos — minus whatever the classifier won't let it say.

A normal frontier model carries its safety in its weights, and Fable keeps that training too. But the decisive safeguard here lives outside the model — a separate network of classifier models, an extension of Anthropic's published Constitutional Classifiers work, described by one IBM researcher as "guardian models on either side" of the Mythos engine.

It runs as a cascade. A cheap first pass screens every exchange and reads the model's own activations to flag risk; suspicious traffic escalates to a heavyweight classifier that judges the prompt and the streaming response together — the kind of layer built to catch a dangerous chemical dressed up as a "food flavoring." When a request touches cybersecurity, biology/chemistry, or model distillation, Fable doesn't refuse: per Anthropic's own announcement it silently re-routes the answer to Opus 4.8 and tells you, on fewer than 5% of sessions, with monitoring and mandatory 30-day retention behind it to catch novel, cross-request attacks.

The effect is blunt and intentional. Ask Fable a genuinely dangerous question — CNBC's example was how to make ricin — and it blocks the engine's answer and hands you Opus 4.8's instead.

You can read the whole design in those two charts. The unrestricted engine is dangerous; the gated public model scores zero on the same tests. As one analyst put it, the center of gravity for safety has shifted from the base model to the classifier layer.

A concrete example of the gate firing: an early tester asked Fable to run a rigorous security audit of a software project. The request tripped the cyber classifier and was handed to Opus 4.8 — which still surfaced several significant issues. Meanwhile, so many ordinary requests got caught that users complained publicly the safeguards were too broad. That friction is the gate doing its job, visibly, every day.

Fable doesn't decline dangerous questions because the engine has good judgment. It declines because something standing in front of the engine intercepts the request.
03 — The Asymmetry

Why a jailbreak in Fable ≠ a jailbreak in Opus

This is the crux the headlines missed.

Jailbreak Opus 4.8, and the prize is Opus-level capability. Even with its safeguards stripped, Opus scores 40% on ExploitBench — roughly the neighborhood of other public models. The ceiling is low because the engine is the limit.

Jailbreak Fable 5's classifier, and you haven't unlocked a slightly better assistant. You've potentially reached the Mythos engine itself — the 78%-ExploitBench model Anthropic deliberately keeps behind vetted access. Same prompt; categorically different worst case. That asymmetry — a thin software gate guarding the most capable cyber engine alive, shipped to hundreds of millions — is the structural thing that alarmed the U.S. government. Not what Fable did. What it could do if the gate failed once.

With Opus, the danger is bounded by the weights. With Fable, the danger is bounded only by whether the gate holds.
04 — The Break

How the gate actually gets beaten

The wrapper is not flimsy. Fable's classifiers extend a defense that, in Anthropic's own tests, cut jailbreak success from 86% to 4.4% and survived a public bug-bounty with no universal jailbreak found. Before launch, more than a thousand hours of red-teaming with the U.S. government and the UK's AI Security Institute turned up nothing universal, and in blocking mode Fable made no progress on offensive-cyber tasks. So how did it fall within days?

Because the gate is strong where attacks used to live — a single malicious prompt — and weak where they're heading: multi-turn and agentic. The headline failure mode has a name. A line of research calls it Internal Safety Collapse (ISC): a model executing an otherwise legitimate, long-horizon task can slide into what the authors call a "zero-safety" state, with the harmful capability emerging from the model's own execution chain rather than from any flaggable input. Frontier models are more exposed, not less — the very skills that let them plan and run multi-step work let them reason their way around their own guardrails. A separate Nature Communications study found reasoning models can even be turned into autonomous jailbreak agents against other models, at a 97% success rate.

Why does that defeat the gate specifically? Because the classifier inspects discrete exchanges for malicious intent — and an agentic task never hands it one.

Notice how cleanly this matches the demonstration Anthropic says it was shown: a "narrow, non-universal" technique that asked the model to read a codebase and fix software flaws — a long-horizon agentic coding task, which is exactly where the wrapper is thinnest.

Within days of launch, a researcher known as "Pliny the Liberator" published a multi-agent run against Fable that combined several of these — obfuscation, long-context dilution, and coordinated agents. The screenshots showed the model producing exploit-development material and chemical-synthesis instructions: the gate held against the blunt question and lost to the distributed one. Two caveats matter for anyone citing it — the screenshots are unverified, and Anthropic has not confirmed this is the bypass that triggered the government's order; its own account points instead to the narrow codebase-audit demonstration above.

None of this surprises Anthropic. Its classifier research states plainly that no defense is perfectly robust and that adversaries keep developing new attacks, and its launch post conceded the safeguards were tuned deliberately strict — strict enough that benign requests sometimes get caught. The wrapper was always a probabilistic bet, not a wall. The fight is over whether that bet is good enough to put the most capable cyber engine on Earth in front of the public.

05 — The Verdict

So who's right?

Anthropic's position is that none of this is what the government feared.

It says the one bypass actually demonstrated to it surfaced only minor, already-known vulnerabilities that Opus 4.8 — and even GPT-5.5 — can find without any bypass at all. No real uplift. By that reading, the wrapper mostly held, and recalling a model used by hundreds of millions over a narrow agentic quirk sets a standard that freezes the entire industry.

The government, citing national security, evidently weighed the structure differently: the most capable cyber engine on Earth, gated by a classifier layer that a long-running agent can route around, deployed to anyone with a login. On that view, "the demonstrated harm was minor" misses the point — the question is what happens the next time the gate is beaten, when the task isn't a benign codebase audit.

The argument the whole field is now having

When the engine is this powerful, is a gate ever enough — or does that much capability belong behind vetting, not a filter?
